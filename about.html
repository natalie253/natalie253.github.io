About me

Hi, I'm Natalie.

Cancer researcher, teacher, and incoming DPhil student at Oxford. I study cancer at the molecular level and believe that rigorous science and genuine human care are not opposites. They are the same thing.

Support my journey
01
Curiosity, then a calling

I have always been drawn to how living things work. Long before I had the vocabulary for it, I was the kind of kid who dissected bugs in the backyard, watched surgical procedures online out of sheer fascination, and found the inner workings of the body genuinely thrilling. Science was not something I had to be talked into. It was just how I naturally engaged with the world.

What gave that curiosity its direction was my mother's cancer diagnosis. I watched our family navigate the fear and uncertainty that illness brings, and I began researching her disease on my own, reading whatever I could find, trying to understand what was happening inside her body. That experience did not create my love of science. It showed me exactly where to aim it.

"You can't ever reach perfection, but you can believe in an asymptote toward which you are ceaselessly striving." Paul Kalanithi

Growing up, neither of my parents graduated from college. My older brother was the first in our family to earn a degree. When it came time for me to pursue higher education, my parents scraped together enough to cover my first semester. The rest was up to me. I worked multiple jobs simultaneously and finished my bachelor's degree in three years, deliberately compressing the timeline to spare myself the cost of a fourth year. I kept moving forward.

02
From undergrad bench to UCSF

My first real research experience came during undergrad at Cal Poly Pomona, where I worked in Dr. Alas's lab studying immune responses to prosthetic debris. I learned flow cytometry, cell culture, antibody staining, and phagocytic assays, the foundational hands-on skills that made everything after possible. Working across five collaborating labs, I also learned that science is a collective project, not a solo one.

When the project stalled due to circumstances outside my control, I did not walk away discouraged. I saw the broader lesson: science requires not just technical skill, but patience, adaptability, and the ability to keep momentum when outcomes are unclear.

From there I moved into a master's program at SFSU, where I began conducting research in partnership with UCSF's Pediatric Oncology Lab. My work focused on osteosarcoma, a bone cancer that primarily affects children and adolescents. Using CUT&RUN, ATAC-seq, and single-cell multiomics, I mapped how transcription factor networks drive tumor aggression. That work contributed to a 2025 publication in Cancer Discovery.

03
Oxford and what comes next

Some of the most important lessons I have learned about medicine came when science had nothing left to offer. He was 24 years old and dying. His parents were not ready to let go, so he endured agonizing pain just to give them a little more time. What mattered most was how his physicians showed up: present, honest, and patient enough to hold space for both clinical reality and love.

That experience stayed with me. I want to become a physician-scientist because I want to help design better questions, not just for experiments, but for people. How do we anticipate which tumors will resist treatment? How do we earn trust while guiding people through the limits of what we can offer?

I have been accepted to Oxford's DPhil program in oncology. At Oxford I will be studying colon cancer, one of the most prevalent and fastest-rising cancers globally, where better molecular understanding of the disease could meaningfully change outcomes for millions of patients. After completing my DPhil, I plan to attend medical school in the United States, so that I can give back not only through research but through patient care, outreach, and clinical work. My long-term goal is to run clinical trials and experimental treatments as a surgeon-scientist, improving outcomes and quality of life for patients directly.

Teaching and mentorship

The part of this work
that keeps me grounded.

Research can feel abstract. Teaching never does. Every student I have worked with has reminded me why the science matters in the first place.

Anatomy with real stakes
Teaching cadaver-based anatomy labs is unlike anything else. Students walk in nervous and walk out with a completely different relationship to the human body. Guiding that shift, and watching someone understand for the first time how a structure they have read about actually sits in space, is one of the most rewarding things I do.
What tutoring taught me about learning
Privately tutoring biology and chemistry forced me to understand not just the material, but how different people get stuck on it. Watching the moment a concept clicks for someone who had convinced themselves they were not a science person changed how I think about teaching entirely. Most people are not bad at science. They just have not had it explained in a way that makes sense to them.
Access as a form of mentorship
Through SEES outreach and peer mentoring, I worked with students who were capable and motivated but operating without the same network others take for granted. The most meaningful thing I could offer was not always content knowledge. It was visibility into what was possible, and someone in their corner who believed they belonged in these spaces.
The long game

Where I'm headed and why it matters.

🔬

DPhil at Oxford in oncology

Three years studying colon cancer at the molecular level at one of the world's leading oncology research institutions. Colon cancer is the 3rd most common cancer globally and the 2nd leading cause of cancer death, with rising rates in adults under 50.

🪚

Medical school in the United States

After the DPhil, I plan to attend medical school in the US. I want to give back not only through research, but through direct patient care and community outreach. The MD and PhD together are the most powerful combination I can bring to the people I want to serve.

🧬

Surgeon-scientist and clinical trials

My long-term goal is to run clinical trials and experimental treatments as a surgeon-scientist, improving outcomes and quality of life for patients directly. I want to sit at the intersection of discovery and care, where the science I do can reach the people who need it most.

🌍

Teaching, mentorship, and access

Throughout everything, I will keep teaching and mentoring. The students I have worked with remind me why this matters beyond the lab. Teaching at a college and making myself accessible as a future physician are goals I take as seriously as my research career.
