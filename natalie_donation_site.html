My story

Science wasn't supposed
to be for me.

I grew up in a recognized cult with strict gender roles, where I was taught that pursuing education or a career would disgrace my family. My purpose, I was told, was to become a homemaker. Science was something I had to hide.

When my mother was diagnosed with cancer, everything changed. I began researching her disease obsessively — and a long-suppressed curiosity reignited. I realized that science wasn't just about solving puzzles. It's about giving people like my mother more time, more options, and more hope.

"You can't ever reach perfection, but you can believe in an asymptote toward which you are ceaselessly striving."

I finished my bachelor's degree in three years, working three jobs simultaneously, to spare myself the cost of a fourth year. I earned my spot through persistence — not connections.

Now I'm an MS researcher at San Francisco State University conducting cancer research at UCSF, teaching anatomy to undergraduates, and applying to MD/PhD programs. This is what it looks like when someone who wasn't supposed to make it, does.
